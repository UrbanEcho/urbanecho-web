Seville is internationally recognised for its bold reallocation of street space, pioneering cycling infrastructure, and vibrant public realm. Through the [ETH Zurich SUTLab](https://im.ibi.ethz.ch/en/research/research-and-thesis-projects1/Sustainable-urban-transitions-lab-sut-lab-pilot-phase.html) programme sponsored by Bolt, SUTLab has deployed a high‑resolution urban digital twin of Hannover on the UrbanEcho Platform. This gives planners, consultants, researchers, and mobility operators a dynamic environment to explore the city’s mobility system, test interventions, and analyse accessibility impacts with unprecedented clarity. One key aspect is that we had to work with limited data and test our algorithms for such cases on this.

## Why Seville

Seville's urban form is defined by a historic, compact centre bounded by the Guadalquivir River and surrounded by rapidly developing suburban districts. The city is renowned for its extensive cycling network and progressive pedestrianisation projects that have reshaped mobility behaviours.

Seasonal and climatic factors, particularly extreme summer temperatures, play a strong role in influencing mobility choices, with usage patterns shifting noticeably between cooler and hotter periods. These features create a unique environment where infrastructure decisions have highly differentiated impacts depending on neighbourhood structure and seasonal conditions.

> **WHY THIS MATTERS ON THE PLATFORM**
>
> Seville's current metro system is limited in coverage, and even small extensions could produce large, spatially uneven accessibility gains, particularly for suburban districts with strong commuting ties to the centre. This makes the city an exceptionally valuable environment for scenario testing on the UrbanEcho platform — especially when combined with MATSim simulations that capture how people actually adjust their travel choices in response to new transit infrastructure.

### Deep dive: metro expansion

Users will be able to explore metro line expansion scenarios and immediately see how each extension reshapes accessibility across neighbourhoods. The digital twin reveals these shifts visually and quantitatively, exposing pockets of new opportunity, emerging demand corridors, and areas where complementary cycling or bus integration becomes essential.

Users can observe:

- Mode shifts when new segments open
- Compare alignment options on equity outcomes
- Benchmark station proposals against simulation
