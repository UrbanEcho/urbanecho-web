Hannover is a polycentric, mid-sized European city with a strong tradition of cycling, well-connected rail services, and a growing commitment to climate-neutral mobility. Through the [ETH Zurich SUTLab](https://im.ibi.ethz.ch/en/research/research-and-thesis-projects1/Sustainable-urban-transitions-lab-sut-lab-pilot-phase.html) programme sponsored by Bolt, the SUTLab has deployed a high‑resolution urban digital twin of Hannover directly onto the UrbanEcho Platform, giving practitioners hands‑on access to explore, test, and analyse the city’s mobility system in a dynamic, data‑rich environment.

### What you can do today

- **Analyse walk and bike accessibility**
  High-resolution indicators combining cycling, pedestrian, and public transport networks.
- **Explore MATSim behavioural patterns**
  Travel demand, mode choices, peak times, and modal shifts with no model setup needed.
- **Upload your own geospatial layers**
  Benchmark zoning plans, infrastructure proposals, and network modifications.
- **Generate AI-powered scenario reports**
  Summaries, comparisons, and shareable narratives via the Echo coplanner.

[Explore the Hannover digital twin on UrbanEcho](/schedule-demo?inline=1)

### Why Hannover

Hannover's compact urban form combined with decentralised sub-centres creates a naturally multimodal metropolitan structure. Strong integration between rail, bus, and cycling networks, a balanced distribution of employment hubs, and growing transit-oriented development make it a sensitive environment for exploring policy-driven transitions.

Because cycling and walking already play a large role in the city's mobility mix, small infrastructure adjustments could produce measurable accessibility effects. The MATSim baseline reveals how behaviour changes across time and space, giving practitioners rare transparency into demand flows.

> **WHY THIS MATTERS ON THE PLATFORM**
>
> When users upload project layers - from proposed cycling corridors to strategic development plans - UrbanEcho automatically benchmarks them against transport simulation outputs. Consultants validate assumptions early, planners test alternatives, and researchers quantify accessibility equity impacts at scale.
